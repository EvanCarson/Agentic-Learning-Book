**Objective:** recognise where RAG breaks and the engineering responses.

## When retrieval goes wrong

RAG does not eliminate hallucination — it relocates the failure point. Instead of the model inventing a fact from training weights, it now cites a retrieved chunk that was irrelevant, stale, or simply wrong. The answer is still confidently delivered. Understanding where the pipeline can fail is a prerequisite for building one you can trust.

## Irrelevant retrieval

The most common failure is retrieving a chunk that looks related but is not actually useful. Keyword overlap and even dense embeddings can surface off-topic passages that share vocabulary with the query. The model, instructed to answer from context, will often extract *something* from an irrelevant chunk rather than admitting it lacks a source. The engineering response is to measure **recall@k**: given a labelled evaluation set, what fraction of queries return a relevant chunk in the top k results? A retrieval layer with poor recall@k will produce wrong answers no matter how good the downstream model is.

## Chunk size mismatches

Chunk size is a first-order knob that fails in both directions. Chunks that are too small lose the surrounding context a passage depends on: a sentence that says "it is the most abundant gas" is meaningless without the sentence before it that names the gas. Chunks that are too large reduce retrieval precision — a 2 000-token chunk containing one relevant sentence and nineteen irrelevant ones will score well for the relevant sentence but dilute the signal fed to the model. Iterating on chunk size against a retrieval quality metric is the correct path; a default of 256–512 tokens with small overlap is a reasonable start, not an answer.

## Stale indices

A retrieval index is only as current as its last update. A product document updated yesterday is invisible to an index built last week. For any domain where correctness depends on recency — pricing, policies, medical guidelines — staleness is a correctness bug, not a performance issue. The engineering response is explicit index freshness tracking and automated re-indexing on source updates.

## Context budget and "lost in the middle"

Passing many chunks into the context window creates two problems. First, there is a hard token budget: every model has a context limit, and retrieved text competes with the query, system prompt, and conversation history. Second, empirical research shows that models reliably use information at the *beginning* and *end* of long contexts but miss information in the middle. Retrieving ten chunks and pasting them all in is not better than retrieving three high-precision chunks — it is often worse. Re-ranking retrieved chunks by relevance before assembly, and keeping the context window lean, are standard mitigations.

## "I don't have a source for that"

A well-designed RAG system makes "I have no supporting source" a first-class answer. When the retriever returns nothing relevant, the correct output is an explicit statement to that effect, not a fallback to parametric knowledge. Training or prompting the model to refuse when it cannot cite is safer than letting it generate an answer that sounds grounded but is not. This also gives you a measurable signal: a high rate of "no source" responses indicates a gap in your corpus, not a model failure.

## Measuring what matters

Retrieval quality (recall@k, mean reciprocal rank) and answer groundedness (is every claim traceable to a retrieved chunk?) are the two metrics that matter for a RAG system. Neither is measured by standard LLM benchmarks. Building lightweight evaluation harnesses for both — even with a small hand-labelled set — is the fastest way to find and fix RAG failures. This connects directly to the Evaluation & Testing module, where systematic measurement of agent behaviour is covered in depth.

> **Best practice:** make "no supporting source" a first-class answer and measure retrieval quality explicitly — assuming the retriever works correctly is how confidently wrong answers get shipped.
